Gaeng Liang — Thai Mixed Vegetable Soup with Fresh Shrimp

⏱ 35 min 🍽 Serves 4 📊 Easy

Gaeng Liang is Thailand's ultimate comfort soup — a light, peppery broth brimming with seasonal vegetables and sweet fresh shrimp. This ancient recipe proves that the simplest dishes are often the most satisfying, delivering maximum flavor with minimal fuss.

💡 Gaeng Liang is one of Thailand's most ancient soups, traditionally made during Buddhist Lent when meat consumption was limited. This nutritious vegetable soup showcases the Thai philosophy of balancing flavors and using whatever fresh vegetables are available seasonally.

🛒 Ingredients

  • 1 medium bottle gourd (about 1 lb), peeled and cut into chunks
  • 7 oz pumpkin, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 5-6 baby corn, halved lengthwise
  • 5 oz fresh shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • 1 bunch Thai basil leaves (about 1 cup packed)
  • 3 shallots, roughly chopped
  • 2 tbsp shrimp paste
  • 1 tsp white peppercorns
  • 4 cups water or light stock
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce
  • 1 tbsp tamarind paste or lime juice

👨‍🍳 Instructions

  1. Using a mortar and pestle, pound the shallots, shrimp paste, and white peppercorns into a rough paste. Don't worry about making it completely smooth — some texture is traditional.
  2. Bring water to a boil in a medium saucepan. Add the pounded paste and stir to dissolve completely.
  3. Add the pumpkin pieces first since they take longest to cook. Simmer for 5 minutes until slightly tender.
  4. Add the bottle gourd and baby corn. Continue simmering for another 5-7 minutes until vegetables are tender but still have some bite.
  5. Add the fresh shrimp and cook for 2-3 minutes until they turn pink and are just cooked through.
  6. Season with fish sauce and tamarind paste, tasting and adjusting as needed. The soup should be mildly salty with a gentle sour note.
  7. Remove from heat and immediately stir in the Thai basil leaves. Serve hot with steamed rice.

💡 Pro Tips

  • Don't skip the mortar and pestle for the paste — the rough texture releases more flavor than a food processor would
  • Add vegetables in order of cooking time needed, starting with the hardest ones first
  • The soup should have a clear, light broth with a gentle peppery heat rather than chili heat
