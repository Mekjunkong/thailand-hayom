Tom Saeb Soft Bone Pork — Spicy Northern Thai Soup with Tender Cartilage

⏱ 60 min 🍽 Serves 3-4 📊 Medium

Tom Saeb is Northern Thailand's answer to those who think they can handle serious heat. This intensely spicy soup features tender pork cartilage simmered until melt-in-your-mouth soft, then fired up with bird's eye chilies and aromatic herbs that'll make you sweat and smile at the same time.

💡 Tom Saeb is a beloved Northern Thai soup known for its intense heat and bold flavors. The soft pork cartilage becomes incredibly tender when slow-cooked, creating a unique texture that's prized in Thai cuisine.

🛒 Ingredients

  • 14 oz pork soft bones (cartilage), cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 8-10 bird's eye chilies
  • 2 limes, juiced
  • 2 tablespoons toasted rice powder (khao kua)
  • 2 stalks lemongrass, bruised and cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 3-4 slices galangal
  • 4-5 kaffir lime leaves, torn
  • 2-3 tablespoons fish sauce, to taste
  • 1-2 teaspoons palm sugar, to taste
  • Fresh Thai basil leaves for garnish
  • 4 cups water

👨‍🍳 Instructions

  1. Bring 4 cups of water to a boil in a medium pot. Add the pork soft bones and simmer for 30-35 minutes until tender and cartilage is soft.
  2. While the pork cooks, pound the bird's eye chilies in a mortar and pestle until roughly crushed. Set aside.
  3. Add the lemongrass, galangal, and kaffir lime leaves to the pot with the pork. Simmer for another 5 minutes to infuse the broth with aromatic herbs.
  4. Season the soup with fish sauce and palm sugar, tasting and adjusting to achieve a balance of salty, spicy, and slightly sweet flavors.
  5. Remove from heat and stir in the crushed chilies, lime juice, and toasted rice powder. The soup should be intensely spicy and tangy.
  6. Ladle into bowls and garnish generously with fresh Thai basil leaves. Serve immediately while hot.

💡 Pro Tips

  • Don't skip the toasted rice powder — it adds a nutty depth and helps thicken the broth slightly
  • Adjust the number of chilies based on your heat tolerance, but this soup is meant to be fiery hot
  • The cartilage should be so tender it almost melts in your mouth — don't rush the cooking process
